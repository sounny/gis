Module 03: Geodesy, Projections & Coordinate Systems

Transforming the curved Earth to flat maps ‚Äî and the trade-offs that come with it.

üåç The Fundamental Challenge

The Earth is approximately spherical ‚Äî yet every map you've ever used is flat. This seemingly simple transformation introduces distortions that affect how we perceive area, shape, distance, and direction.

In this module, you'll learn how geodesy (the science of measuring Earth) and map projections work together to solve this ancient problem ‚Äî and why every projection is a compromise.

üìê Core Concepts

üåê

Geodesy

The science of accurately measuring and representing the Earth's shape, gravitational field, and rotation. Modern geodesy relies on satellites, GPS, and precise mathematical models.

üìç

Datum

A mathematical model of the Earth used as a reference for coordinates. Common datums include WGS84 (used by GPS), NAD83 (North America), and NAVD88 (vertical datum).

üó∫Ô∏è

Map Projection

A systematic transformation of the Earth's 3D surface onto a 2D plane. Every projection preserves some properties (area, shape, distance, direction) while distorting others.

üìè

Coordinate System

Geographic coordinates use latitude/longitude on a sphere. Projected coordinates (like UTM or State Plane) use X/Y on a flat surface with units in meters or feet.

üìñ Case Study from History: Eratosthenes Measures the Earth (240 BCE)

Over 2,200 years ago, the Greek mathematician Eratosthenes calculated Earth's circumference with remarkable accuracy using just a stick, a well, and the shadow of the sun.

He observed that at noon on the summer solstice, the sun shone directly into a well in Syene (modern Aswan, Egypt), while in Alexandria, a vertical stick cast a shadow. By measuring the shadow's angle (~7.2¬∞) and knowing the distance between the cities (~5,000 stadia), he estimated the Earth's circumference to within 2% of its actual value.

Why this matters: This ancient technique demonstrates the core of geodesy ‚Äî using geometric principles to understand our planet's shape.
