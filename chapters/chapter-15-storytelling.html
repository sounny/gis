Throughout this course, you've learned to analyze data. Now, you must learn to communicate it. A "Story Map" combines interactive maps with multimedia content‚Äîtext, photos, video, and audio‚Äîto tell a story about the world.

1. The Hook

Every good story needs a beginning. In GIS, this is your "Overview Map." You establish context. Where are we? Why should the reader care?

"It was a dark and stormy night in the Florida Keys..."

2. The Conflict

Zoom in to the problem. Show the data that reveals the tension. Maybe it's rising sea levels, or a gap in service delivery.

Notice how the map successfully directed your eye to the specific island in question? You didn't have to search for it.

3. The Resolution

Present the analysis. This is where your colorful Choropleth or Heat Map shines. Show the solution or the future projection.

A Story Map isn't just a data dump; it guides the user to a conclusion.

üö´ The "Bad Maps" Gallery

Just because you can map it, doesn't mean you should. Here are common storytelling mistakes from Dr. Sounny's "Bad Maps" lecture.

üåà
The Rainbow Bomb

Using 50 different bright colors for 50 US States. It creates visual chaos and implies meaning where there is none. Use a muted palette!

üìä
Map Junk

Filling every inch of white space with north arrows, massive logos, and clip art. Less is more. Let the data breathe.

ü§î
The "Where's Waldo?"

Making the reader hunt for the subject. If your map is about "Hospitals", mapping "Roads" in bright red and hospitals in pale grey fails the story.

üìà
Normalization Fail

Mapping raw population counts instead of rates. This usually just results in a map of "Where People Live" rather than your actual topic.
