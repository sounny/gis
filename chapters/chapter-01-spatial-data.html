The Two Pillars of GIS

In the digital realm, geographic information is predominantly stored in one of two formats: Vector or Raster. Choosing the right one is the fundamental decision of every GIS project.

Vector Model

Represents the world as discrete objects. Think of this as "connect the dots" or architectural drafting.

  • Points: Exact X,Y coordinates (e.g., a specific tree).
  • Lines: Connected sequences of points (e.g., a section of Interstate 10).
  • Polygons: Closed areas (e.g., the boundary of Travis County).
Best for features with clear, distinct boundaries.

▦ Raster Model

Represents the world as a continuous grid of cells. Think of this as a digital photograph or a TV screen.

  • Pixels: Each cell contains a single value (e.g., elevation or color).
  • Resolution: The size of the cell determines the detail (e.g., 30m vs 1m pixels).
  • Surfaces: Ideal for continuous phenomena like temperature or elevation.
Best for imagery and continuous environmental data.

Summary of Big Ideas

  • Spatial data is information that has a specific location on Earth's surface.
  • The Vector model uses discrete geometry (points, lines, polygons) to represent features.
  • The Raster model uses a grid of equal-sized cells to represent continuous phenomena.
  • The GIS Sandwich is the conceptual stacking of these layers to perform analysis.

Glossary

Geometric Primitive:
The three basic vector shapes: points, lines, and polygons.
Resolution:
In raster GIS, the dimension of the area on the ground represented by a single pixel.
Topology:
The mathematical study of spatial relationships (overlap, adjacency, connectivity).

Important Person

Roger Tomlinson (1933–2014)

Known as the "Father of GIS," Tomlinson was a visionary who developed the Canada Geographic Information System in the 1960s—the world's first computerized GIS. He realized that while paper maps were static, digital data could be layered and analyzed mathematically.

Local to Global

The Texas Parcel Map

In Texas, the General Land Office (GLO) and local appraisals districts manage one of the most complex vector datasets in the country: Land Parcels. Every fence line and property boundary in the state is a polygon with detailed ownership attributes.

Globally, these patterns are mirrored in "Cadastral Maps" used for taxation and urban planning across the world.
