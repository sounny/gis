Chapter 01: What is Spatial Data?

The digital world is built on two primary ways of representing geography: Points, lines, and polygons (Vector) or grids of pixels (Raster).

Two Ways to View the World

Geographic Information Systems (GIS) rely on digital representations of the real world. While reality is infinitely complex, we simplify it into two data models:

üìç Vector Data

Discrete objects defined by coordinates.

  • Points: Trees, hydrants, poles.
  • Lines: Roads, rivers, pipelines.
  • Polygons: Parcels, lakes, counties.
Best for: Human-made features with clear boundaries.

‚ñ¶ Raster Data

Continuous grids of values (pixels).

  • Imagery: Satellite photos, aerial drone maps.
  • Elevation: Digital Elevation Models (DEMs).
  • Temperature: Interpolated surface heat maps.
Best for: Continuous phenomena without hard edges.

The "Sandwich" Model of GIS

A GIS project is often visualized as a "sandwich" of layers. The bottom layer might be a raster base map (satellite imagery), overlaid with vector layers like roads (lines) and landmarks (points).

Key Takeaway: Vector data is scalable and precise, while raster data is rich in detail but file-size heavy. Knowing when to use each is the first skill of a GIS analyst.
