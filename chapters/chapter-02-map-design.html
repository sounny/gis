Chapter 02: Map Elements & Design

A map is not just data—it is a communication tool. Good design ensures your message is received clearly and accurately.

The T.O.L.S. Principle

Every professional map should contain five key elements to be considered complete. Remember the acronym T.O.L.S. (sometimes taught as TALLS):

  • T - Title: Descriptive and concise. distinct from the subtitle.
  • O - Orientation: A north arrow or graticule to show direction.
  • L - Legend: Defines symbols and colors used on the map.
  • S - Scale: A scale bar or representative fraction (1:24,000).
  • S - Source: Credits for the data providers (e.g., "Source: US Census Bureau").

Visual Hierarchy

Visual hierarchy is the arrangement of elements in a way that implies importance. The most important information (the subject of the map) should be the most prominent.

Common Mistake: Do not let the base map overpower your data! If you are mapping schools, the schools should be bright and bold, while the street background should be muted or gray.

Figure-Ground Relationship

Good contrast helps the eye distinguish the "figure" (your subject) from the "ground" (the background). Use color, brightness, and size to achieve this separation.
