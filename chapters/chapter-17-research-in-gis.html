Up to this point, you have been learning how to use GIS tools to replicate existing workflows or analyze provided datasets. Research is the next step: using these tools to answer a question that has not been answered before.

Whether you are analyzing the impact of remote work on urban design or measuring environmental sustainability, the research process remains the same.

üåç Major Research Themes

As you begin your journey as a researcher, consider these emerging themes in modern geography.

Placemaking at Work

How do we create meaningful workplaces in the age of Remote and Hybrid work?

  • Architectural History: From Taylorism (efficiency) to Holism (well-being).
  • Productivity: Does the physical environment of an office actually improve output?
  • The "Third Place": Mapping co-working spaces and coffee shops as new hubs of labor.

Sustainability & Impact

Using the IPAT Equation (Impact = Population √ó Affluence √ó Technology) to measure our footprint.

  • Remote Work Impact: Does working from home reduce carbon emissions (less commuting) or increase them (heating individual homes)?
  • Smart Buildings: Using IoT sensors to turn off HVAC in empty zones.
  • Urban Heat: Analyzing how green roofs reduce energy demand.

üìö The Literature Review

Before you open ArcGIS Pro, you must open a book (or a PDF). A literature review establishes the context for your work.

Pro Tip: Use tools like Google Scholar or your university library. Look for "Review Articles" to get a broad overview of a topic quickly.

Example: The Evolution of Workplace Design

If you were researching "Placemaking," you would trace the history from the rigid rows of the early 20th-century factory (Taylorism) to the open-plan offices of the 2000s, and finally to the decentralized "Smart Office" of today.
