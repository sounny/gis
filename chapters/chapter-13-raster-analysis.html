Because raster data is essentially a matrix of numbers, we can perform mathematical operations on maps. This framework, developed by Dana Tomlin, treats geographic layers as variables in an equation.

ðŸ”‘ The Equation: Input Layer + Function = Output Layer.

The Four Scopes

Raster operations are categorized by how many cells they "look at" to calculate an answer:

  • Local: Uses only the cell at the same location (Cell-by-cell).
  • Focal: Uses a neighborhood of cells (e.g., 3x3 window).
  • Zonal: Uses all cells within a specific region or "zone."
  • Global: Considers every cell in the entire map.

Summary of Big Ideas

  • Reclassification allows us to simplify complex values into simple ranks (e.g., 1-10).
  • Weighted Overlay combines multiple factors with different levels of importance.
  • Euclidean Distance calculates the straight-line distance to the nearest source.
  • Surface Analysis (Slope, Aspect) is a focal operation derived from elevation data.

Map Algebra:
A mathematical language for raster analysis where layers are treated as numbers.
Neighborhood:
The set of cells surrounding a focal cell (often a 3x3 or 5x5 moving window).
Mask:
A raster layer used to define areas of interest (AOI) where analysis should be performed.

Important Person

C. Dana Tomlin

A Professor at the University of Pennsylvania and Yale, Dana Tomlin is the pioneer who developed **Map Algebra**. In the 1980s, he created the GIS software "MAP" which introduced the local, focal, zonal, and global operations that are now standard in every GIS system on the planet.

Local to Global

Flood Analysis in Houston

In the wake of Hurricane Harvey, Houston engineers used raster analysis to model how water flows across the city. By performing **Focal Flow Direction** and **Zonal Accumulation** on high-resolution LiDAR data, they can identify the exact "Cost Paths" where water will collect in neighborhoods along Buffalo Bayou.

This "math on maps" is the foundation for the billion-dollar flood mitigation projects protecting the Texas coast.
