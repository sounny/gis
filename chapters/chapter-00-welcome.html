üöÄ Getting Started with GIS Software

Multi-Platform Learning: While this book uses ArcGIS Pro and Google Earth Engine as primary tools, we also explore open-source alternatives like QGIS and accessible Web GIS platforms.

ArcGIS Pro Setup

ArcGIS Pro is the industry-leading desktop GIS software from Esri. You'll use this for most of your hands-on exercises involving vector analysis, cartography, and spatial modeling.

System Requirements: ArcGIS Pro requires Windows 10/11 (64-bit). Mac users can use a virtual machine, Boot Camp, or access through cloud-based solutions.

QGIS (Open Source Alternative)

QGIS is a powerful, free, and open-source desktop GIS application. It runs on Windows, Mac, and Linux. While our primary exercises use ArcGIS Pro, QGIS is an excellent alternative for readers who:

  • Use Mac or Linux operating systems natively.
  • Want a completely free professional GIS suite.
  • Wish to develop skills in the open-source geospatial ecosystem.

Google Earth Engine (Cloud RS)

For remote sensing and large-scale imagery analysis, we'll use Google Earth Engine‚Äîa cloud-based platform that provides access to petabytes of satellite imagery and geospatial datasets.

Free Access: Google Earth Engine is freely available for research, education, and non-profit uses. Sign up at earthengine.google.com.

Web GIS Platforms

Beyond desktop software, we will leverage modern Web GIS tools to share our work and perform lightweight analysis directly in the browser:

  • ArcGIS Online: For creating interactive web maps, StoryMaps, and dashboard visualizations.
  • Kepler.gl / Unfolded: For high-performance visualization of large spatial datasets.
  • Google My Maps: Simple custom map creation for introductory projects.
  • Sounny.github.io: Collection of online GIS tools and demos.

üí° Tips for Success

  • Establish a regular reading and practice routine. Consistency is key when learning complex spatial concepts and software workflows.
  • Read through the material thoroughly. This textbook contains numerous tips and "pro-tips" that can save you hours of troubleshooting.
  • Engage with the GIS community. Join online forums like GIS Stack Exchange or Esri Community to see how professionals solve problems.
  • Don't skip the hands-on exercises. Theory is important, but GIS is a craft that is best learned by doing.
  • Work through chapters incrementally. Each chapter builds on the concepts introduced in the previous ones.
  • Back up your spatial data. GIS projects can be complex; always keep backups of your work on a cloud service or external drive.

üìñ Glossary Preview

Here are some key terms you'll encounter throughout this book:

GIS (Geographic Information System)
A system designed to capture, store, manipulate, analyze, manage, and present spatial or geographic data.
Remote Sensing
The acquisition of information about an object or phenomenon without making physical contact, typically using satellite or aerial sensors.
Vector Data
Geographic data represented as points, lines, and polygons with associated attributes.
Raster Data
Geographic data represented as a grid of cells (pixels), commonly used for imagery and continuous phenomena.
Spatial Analysis
The process of examining the locations, attributes, and relationships of features in spatial data through techniques such as overlay and proximity analysis.
