‚öñÔ∏è Critical GIS: Maps are Power

We often think of maps as objective "truth." But every map is a series of choices: what to show, what to hide, and how to classify reality. Critical GIS challenges us to ask:

  • Who made this map? (Funding, bias)
  • Who is missing? (Marginalized communities often under-mapped)
  • What is the intent? (Persuasion vs. Information)
Key Concept: Jerrymandering
The most famous example of "Weaponized GIS." Using the exact same population data, politicians can draw district lines to guarantee a win. This is technically accurate GIS, but ethically questionable.

ü§ñ GeoAI: The New Frontier

Artificial Intelligence is transforming GIS from "Computer Aided Design" to "Computer Aided Decision." We now use Deep Learning to automate tasks that used to take months.

Object Detection

Training a model to find swimming pools, solar panels, or damaged roofs in satellite imagery.

detect_objects(input_raster, model="pool_finder.dlpk")

Pixel Classification

Semantic segmentation. Automatically classifying land cover (Forest vs. Urban) at scale.

classify_pixels(sentinel_img, schema="land_cover")

Prediction

Using Regression to predict future events, like crop yields or crime hotspots.

predict_regression(variables, target="yield")

The "Black Box" Problem

AI models are often opaque. If an algorithm denies a loan based on "location risk," but we don't know exactly why the AI decided that, we perpetuate systemic bias without accountability.

Scenario: The Outbreak

You are a GIS Analyst for the County Health Department during a viral outbreak. You have a dataset of confirmed positive cases with home addresses. The Governor asks for a public map to "show people where the danger is."

‚ùå Ethical Violation!

Mapping individual patients violates medical privacy (HIPAA) and endangers them. People might harass households marked as "infected."

Lesson: Always aggregate sensitive personal data.

‚úÖ Good Choice!

Aggregating to ZIP Code or Census Tract protects individual privacy while still showing the spatial pattern of the outbreak.
